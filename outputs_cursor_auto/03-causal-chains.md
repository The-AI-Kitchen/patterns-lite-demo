# Lens 3: Causal Chains — Real Gig Work Transcripts

Only chains where the participant explicitly linked a trigger to a result are included (markers: "because," "so I," "that led me to," "if I don't, then," "after that I started," "that's why," "due to"). Pain points and emotion are out of scope for this lens. Chains within a transcript appear in order of appearance.

## A1

### Chain 1 — Workaround (assist driver instead of solo delivery)
- Trigger: his Android phone is not working for solo delivery and he can't afford an iPhone on this pay.
- Action: rides along as an assistant to truck/Flex drivers and does the hand-offs.
- Result: he keeps earning hours despite the phone gap.
- Type: Workaround.
- Anchoring quote: [A1: "But I have been assistant to truck drivers with"]

### Chain 2 — Habit formation (5-year tenure tied to SSI)
- Trigger: SSI/disability income supplements the part-time Amazon pay.
- Action: stays in the part-time role rather than leaving for full-time work.
- Result: a five-year tenure built on income stacking.
- Type: Habit formation.
- Anchoring quote: [A1: "That's one reason I've been in for five years because"]

### Chain 3 — Switching (planned exit toward law enforcement)
- Trigger: "the treatment" he gets in low-wage jobs.
- Action: starts thinking about becoming a cop or working for the government.
- Result: framing this Amazon role as something he plans to leave.
- Type: Switching (planned).
- Anchoring quote: [A1: "I'm tired with the treatment. So, I'm thinking about just becoming"]

### Chain 4 — Habit formation (entry via a stranger at Quick Check)
- Trigger: a man at the Quick Check at the train station told him to drive for Amazon.
- Action: he applied for it.
- Result: "ever since I did, I stuck with it" — the recommendation became the entry point and the reason he stayed.
- Type: Habit formation.
- Anchoring quote: [A1: "I was actually talking to somebody at Quick Check"]

### Chain 5 — Coping (silence at work to avoid being fired)
- Trigger: coworkers tell on each other for extra hours and theft is around.
- Action: he refuses to talk to anyone on shift.
- Result: he stays out of trouble and keeps his hours.
- Type: Coping.
- Anchoring quote: [A1: "I don't talk to nobody. Because you can get"]

### Chain 6 — Coping (stay out of drama to protect housing money)
- Trigger: he needs the small paycheck to keep paying for a roughly $100 room.
- Action: avoids coworker drama and theft talk.
- Result: he keeps both the job and the room.
- Type: Coping.
- Anchoring quote: [A1: "I have more to lose than somebody who don't"]

### Chain 7 — Workaround / Habit formation (the three-photo ritual)
- Trigger: people can walk onto any porch and take a package.
- Action: makes a routine of photographing the package in hand, the address, and the drop spot.
- Result: photo + timestamp becomes the proof when customers claim non-delivery.
- Type: Workaround / Habit formation.
- Anchoring quote: [A1: "That's why you make sure you take the pictures."]

## Cross-transcript chain patterns

Patterns that recur across two or more transcripts, each supported by the participant's own causal language. Each citation below is anchored to a chain above.

### Pattern A — Physical decline or injury → end of delivery work
Three participants explicitly attribute the end (or planned end) of their delivery work to bodily wear or injury.

- A4: health went down, so he stopped doing deliveries — [A4: "My health has gone down, so I don't do"]
- A5: winter ice → shoulder injury → four-day delivery stint ended — [A5: "I had started in the middle of winter and during one deliver"]
- A11: van entry/exit → sciatica and knees → she split from Amazon — [A11: "I had to split due to a lot of"]

### Pattern B — Wait-for-surge as the per-block pay strategy
Two Flex drivers describe the same explicit cause-and-effect: blocks pay more in surge, so they refuse the standard offer and wait.

- A3: waits for high-demand blocks because pay jumps from ~$40 to ~$90 — [A3: "So I pick those because the pay is like"]
- A10: waits past the $80 offer for surge slots because she has another job and doesn't need to take the first one — [A10: "Yeah, I usually do. I'll just wait. Because I"]

### Pattern C — Word-of-mouth recommendation → applied → stuck with the gig
Three participants attribute their entry to the platform to a specific person who pointed them at it.

- A1: a man at the Quick Check at the train station told him to drive for Amazon and he "stuck with it" — [A1: "I was actually talking to somebody at Quick Check"]
- A4: a fellow Uber Eats driver mentioned Amazon Flex mid-run, so he jumped over to the website and applied — [A4: "I ran into a fellow driver out there and"]
- A6: an acquaintance already doing it told him he could "make what you want to make," so he did exactly that — [A6: "I do know someone who was actually doing it"]

### Pattern D — App-suggested order overridden by a self-built route
Two participants explicitly say the app's order is not enforced (or not sensible) and respond by routing the deliveries themselves.

- A6: he manually re-groups packages by zip code and then by quadrant (NW/SW/NE/SE) before leaving the facility — [A6: "When I go inside, what I do is I"]
- A10: she ignores the app's order, looks at the map, and builds her own route — [A10: "You can see them on the map, and you"]

### Pattern E — Manager/support vacuum → personal workaround layer
Three participants explicitly built a private workaround to compensate for the lack of an accessible manager or unreliable platform support.

- A7: kept a paper list of phone numbers in the truck because "you got to call somebody or you got to get online" through "automation shit" — [A7: "I always kept a list of the phone numbers"]
- A10: leans on Facebook driver groups and on her hospital income because Amazon "will deactivate your account at the drop of a dime" without explanation — [A10: "they'll deactivate your account at the drop of a"]
- A11: when the rabbit servers crashed for three days, drivers fell back onto their personal phones for scans and photos — [A11: "And one day servers were down for everybody. These"]
