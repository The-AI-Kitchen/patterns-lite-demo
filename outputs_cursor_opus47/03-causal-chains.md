# Lens 3: Causal Chains — Real Gig Work Transcripts

Each chain below traces an explicit causal sequence the participant stated (markers: "because," "so I," "that led me to," "if I don't, then," "after that I started"). Pain points and emotion are out of scope for this lens. Chains follow the order they appear in each transcript.

---

## A1

### Chain 1 — Workaround (no iPhone)
- Trigger: his Android phone "is not working" for solo delivery and he can't afford an iPhone on his pay.
- Action: works as an assistant riding with a truck/Flex driver instead of doing solo delivery.
- Result: still earns hours, but only in a support role.
- Type: Workaround.
- Anchoring quote and citation: [A1: "But I have been assistant to truck drivers with..."]

### Chain 2 — Habit formation (stay-and-tolerate)
- Trigger: alternate income (SSI from father's death) covers part of his needs.
- Action: stays in the part-time Amazon role despite poor hours/pay.
- Result: has held the job for five years.
- Type: Habit formation.
- Anchoring quote and citation: [A1: "That's one reason I've been in for five years because..."]

### Chain 3 — Switching (planned exit)
- Trigger: minimum-wage treatment in low-wage jobs.
- Action: starts considering law enforcement / a government job.
- Result: framing this Amazon role as something he is leaving for a different field.
- Type: Switching (planned).
- Anchoring quote and citation: [A1: "I'm thinking about just becoming a cop or something,..."]

### Chain 4 — Habit formation (job entry)
- Trigger: a stranger at the Quick Check at the train station told him to drive for Amazon.
- Action: applied.
- Result: "ever since I did, I stuck with it" — the recommendation became his entry point and the reason he kept the job alongside SSI.
- Type: Habit formation.
- Anchoring quote and citation: [A1: "I was actually talking to somebody at Quick Check..."]

### Chain 5 — Coping (silence at work)
- Trigger: peers telling on each other for extra hours, plus theft accusations floating around.
- Action: refuses to talk to anyone at work.
- Result: avoids being fired or pulled into trouble.
- Type: Coping.
- Anchoring quote and citation: [A1: "I don't talk to nobody. Because you can get..."]

### Chain 6 — Coping (avoiding drama)
- Trigger: needs the small paycheck to pay a roughly $100 room.
- Action: stays out of coworkers' drama and theft talk.
- Result: keeps the job and the housing money.
- Type: Coping.
- Anchoring quote and citation: [A1: "I have more to lose than somebody who don't..."]

### Chain 7 — Workaround (delivery proof)
- Trigger: people can walk onto any porch and steal a package.
- Action: makes sure to take the package, address, and porch photos every drop.
- Result: photo + timestamp serves as the proof when customers claim non-delivery.
- Type: Workaround / Habit formation.
- Anchoring quote and citation: [A1: "That's why you make sure you take the pictures...."]

## Cross-transcript chain patterns

Patterns that recur across two or more transcripts, each supported by participant-stated causal links.

### Pattern A — Physical decline → abandonment of delivery work
Three participants explicitly tie the end (or planned end) of their delivery work to bodily wear or injury.

- A4: health → no more deliveries — [A4: "My health has gone down, so I don't do..."]
- A5: winter ice → shoulder injury → 4-day delivery stint ended — [A5: "I had started in the middle of winter and..."]
- A11: van entry/exit → sciatica + knees → left Amazon — [A11: "I had to split due to a lot of..."]

### Pattern B — Wait-for-surge as a per-block pay strategy
Two Flex drivers describe the same explicit cause-and-effect: blocks pay more at surge, so they wait instead of taking the first offer.

- A3: waits for high-demand blocks because pay jumps from ~$40 to ~$90 — [A3: "So I pick those because the pay is like..."]
- A10: waits for last-minute 4 AM / 5 PM blocks because rates rise when drivers are short — [A10: "Yeah, I usually do. I'll just wait. Because I..."]

### Pattern C — Word-of-mouth recommendation → applied → started gig work
Three participants attribute their entry to the platform to a specific person who pointed them at it.

- A1: a man at the Quick Check at the train station told him to drive for Amazon, and he "stuck with it" — [A1: "I was actually talking to somebody at Quick Check..."]
- A4: a fellow Uber Eats driver mentioned Amazon Flex on a run, so he jumped to the website and applied — [A4: "I ran into a fellow driver out there and..."]
- A6: an acquaintance who was already doing it told him "you can basically make what you want to make," and he signed on — [A6: "I do know someone who was actually doing it..."]

### Pattern D — Manager vacuum → personal workaround layer
Two participants explicitly built a private workaround to compensate for the lack of an accessible manager / unreliable platform support.

- A7: kept a paper list of phone numbers in the truck because "you got to call somebody or you got to get online" through "automation shit" — [A7: "I always kept a list of the phone numbers..."]
- A10: leans on Facebook driver groups and on her hospital income because Amazon "will deactivate your account at the drop of a dime" without explanation — [A10: "they'll deactivate your account at the drop of a..."]
